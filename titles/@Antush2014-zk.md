> [!abstract]
> The essay argues that the corporate “Ed Deform” movement endangers public education and teachers’ working conditions by redirecting funding to standardized testing, curriculum packages, and technology while undermining teachers’ professional judgment. New York’s Advance/APPR evaluation, mandated under Race to the Top, uses student test scores and observation frameworks like Danielson to quantify teacher “productivity” and enforce conformity. The Common Core and associated tests and curricular modules centralize the conception of instruction, constraining teachers’ ability to tailor lessons or implement culturally relevant pedagogy. The author frames these changes as modern Taylorism—dissociating skill from labor, separating conception from execution, and dictating tasks—drawing on Marx and Braverman and citing historical examples like certification test purges and Bloomberg’s mayoral control. The result has been punitive micromanagement, job losses or demotions, and a narrowed curriculum that disproportionately harms teachers and students in marginalized communities.
