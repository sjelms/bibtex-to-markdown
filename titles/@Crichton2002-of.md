> [!bibliography]
> Crichton, Michael. 2002. “Why Speculate?.” 

> [!abstract]
> Michael Crichton argues that modern media is dominated by speculation about the future rather than reporting facts, and he will deliberately speculate about why that is. He defines media broadly and criticizes speculative reporting as useless because nobody truly knows the future, illustrating how the Gell-Mann Amnesia effect leads people to grant media undeserved credibility. He rejects the idea that pundits, specialists, or futurists can reliably predict outcomes, citing failed forecasts on issues like population, environmental collapse, welfare reform, and policy effects. Crichton explains why media and academia favor speculation: it is cheap, generates attention in the present, is career‑protecting when untestable, and benefits from public forgetfulness. Finally, he warns that speculation produces excess, spectacle, and crisis‑mongering that distort public understanding and debate.
