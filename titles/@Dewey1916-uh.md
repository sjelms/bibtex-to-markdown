> [!bibliography]
> Dewey, John. 1916. “Democracy and Education: An Introduction to the Philosophy of Education.” [[Macmillan]]

> [!abstract]
> Dewey argues in Democracy and Education that education is a structural necessity of social life, arising from the continual replacement of mature members with immature ones. New members of a society are born without the knowledge, skills, and commitments required to sustain the group, creating a fundamental gap between generations. Education functions to bridge this gap by initiating the young into the shared practices, purposes, and understandings of the community. As societies become more complex, this gap widens, making informal growth and basic survival skills insufficient for social continuity. Deliberate and sustained educational effort is therefore required to transmit accumulated cultural knowledge and to cultivate active engagement with social aims. For Dewey, education is the primary mechanism through which a society reproduces and maintains its characteristic way of life.
