> [!abstract]
> Marx and Marxists have presumed the development of the labour process under capitalism to involve a progressive rise in direct managerial control (primarily through extending the division of labour and through mechanisation). Harry Braverman (1974, ch. 4) in particular, has argued that the organisation of work during twentieth century capitalism has been guided by Taylorian principles which would ideally involve the control of all worker time and movement by management through complete specification in advance of all aspects of work tasks to be performed. This view arises from a neglect of worker resistance as a force causing accommodating changes in che capitalist mode of production, rather than simply a force which might eventually overthrow that mode of production. In this paper some implications of explicitly recognising worker resistance and managerial counter-pressure as a process by which the organisation of work itself is moulded are explored. In section I treatment of worker resistance and managerial counter-pressure by Marx and Marxists is briefly discussed. In section Il the word control is examined briefly to clear up some confusion and to clarify what I mean by direct control. The basic managerial strategies (responsible autonomy and direct control) are examined in relation to the functions of management under capitalism in section III. Finally, in section IV the implementation of these strategies in practice is discussed.
