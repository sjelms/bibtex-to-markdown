> [!bibliography]
> Geertz, Clifford J. 1973. “Thick Description: Towards an Interpretive Theory of Culture.” [[Basic Books]]

> [!abstract]
> In this chapter, Clifford Geertz develops a foundational argument for interpretive anthropology by redefining culture as a system of publicly shared meanings expressed through symbolic action. Rejecting both reductionist behavioral explanations and purely psychological accounts of culture, Geertz proposes a semiotic conception of culture, drawing on Max Weber’s notion that humans are suspended in “webs of significance” they themselves have spun. Anthropological inquiry, therefore, should not seek universal laws but instead pursue interpretive understanding of meaning within specific social contexts. Central to this approach is the methodological concept of “thick description,” which distinguishes between superficial observation of behavior and the interpretation of its layered social meanings. Through illustrative examples—including Gilbert Ryle’s famous “wink versus twitch” analogy and a detailed ethnographic narrative from Morocco—Geertz demonstrates how ethnographic analysis involves interpreting multiple overlapping structures of signification. Ethnography, he argues, is fundamentally an interpretive act: a process of inscribing social discourse and rendering it analytically accessible. Geertz concludes that cultural analysis operates through interpretive inference rather than predictive theory, aiming to illuminate how symbolic forms organize human experience. The broader contribution of interpretive anthropology, therefore, lies in expanding the scope of human discourse by making diverse systems of meaning intelligible.
