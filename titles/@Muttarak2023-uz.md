> [!abstract]
> The future of work, according to recent reports from the ILO and OECD and from consulting groups like McKinsey, is one in which a substantial proportion of today's jobs could be made obsolete by technological change. The 2019 World Development Report, focused on the changing nature of work, was more sanguine, maintaining that “technology is disrupting the demand for skills” and displacing much routine work, but seeing the overall employment effect as likely positive. (The predictions of job losses the Report cites range widely.) The disruption, however, is the immediate, first-order effect, exemplified by the spread of automation and robotics through the manufacturing and distribution sectors in recent decades and, prospectively, by the incursions of artificial intelligence into areas where cognitive skills had once promised a degree of immunity. The displacement picture is complicated by the simultaneous experience of offshoring to lower-cost countries, especially from the United States, Japan, and Western Europe.
