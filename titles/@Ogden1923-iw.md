> [!abstract]
> Much about language continues to be only hazily understood, distorted by our habitual attitude—often one of indifference—toward words, or by lingering assumptions based on discredited theories. What IS the relationship between words and what the words refer to? Between words and the way we think? Can understanding such matters lead to greater precision in communication? Readers considering these questions find themselves at the crossroads of linguistics and communications theory, of literary criticism and philosophy—an interdisciplinary nexus claimed by the increasingly influential field of semiotics—and "The Meaning of Meaning" will prove, as it has over the last six decades, an essential resource. Published in 1923, C.K. Ogden and I.A. Richards’ foundational text, The Meaning of Meaning, revolutionizes linguistics and semiotics by asserting that words possess no inherent meaning. Central to their argument is the "Semantic Triangle," a psychological model illustrating that meaning emerges only through the mediated interaction between a symbol, a mental reference, and a real-world referent. By debunking the "Proper Meaning Superstition," the authors demonstrate that the connection between language and objects is indirect and entirely dependent on subjective context and individual experience. The work further categorizes language into symbolic and emotive functions—distinguishing between factual information and the evocation of feeling—while warning that miscommunication stems from a failure to recognize the fluid, interpretative nature of symbols. Ultimately, the text serves as a critical study of ambiguity, emphasizing that effective communication requires a disciplined management of the contexts in which language is deployed and interpreted.
