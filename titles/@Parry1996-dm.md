> [!abstract]
> Training professionals can justify the cost of training if they can measure the return on investment (ROI) of this function. Unfortunately, very few training departments try to measure the results of training because of the many difficulties this task poses. Training's ROI is hard to quantify because its benefits are subjective and not easily converted into dollars, trainers lack the necessary accounting skills, and the inconvenience for managers of collecting additional data on training results. However, there are strong arguments for persisting in quantifying training's return on investment, including justification of HR budgets, improved performance of the HR staff and improved training programs. Training ROI can be measured by analyzing hard data such as time saved or increased sales volumes; through estimates by trainees and their managers; by conducing cost/benefit analysis via accounting; and by assessing the results of action plans and managers' briefings.
