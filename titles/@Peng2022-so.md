> [!abstract]
> The relationship between workplace stress and health outcomes has been widely examined, with evidence suggesting that certain forms of occupational stress contribute to elevated risks of cardiovascular disease and premature mortality. Research from the Whitehall studies of British civil servants demonstrated that the most harmful forms of job stress are not necessarily associated with high levels of responsibility or decision-making authority, but rather with limited autonomy and low levels of control over work activities. Employees occupying lower-status roles, who had little influence over how or when tasks were performed, experienced significantly higher mortality rates than those in positions with greater discretion and authority. These findings challenge conventional assumptions that high-pressure executive roles are inherently the most damaging. Instead, the research indicates that the combination of high demands and restricted control over one’s work environment may represent a particularly significant occupational health risk.
