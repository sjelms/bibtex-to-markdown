> [!bibliography]
> Schön, Donald A. 1995. "Knowing-in-action: The new scholarship requires a new epistemology." Vol. 27. [[Informa UK Limited]]. http://dx.doi.org/10.1080/00091383.1995.10544673

> [!abstract]
> The emergence of what Ernest Boyer describes as “new forms of scholarship” necessitates a reconsideration of the epistemological foundations underpinning the modern research university. While the scholarship of discovery has traditionally been privileged as the central and most authoritative form of academic inquiry, Boyer’s framework expands this conception to include the scholarship of integration, application, and teaching. These forms seek not only to generate new knowledge but to contextualize existing knowledge, apply it to consequential problems, and transform it through pedagogical practice. For these domains to be recognized as legitimate forms of scholarship, they must produce knowledge that meets standards of validity, rigor, and openness to critical scrutiny within scholarly communities. This raises fundamental questions regarding the nature of knowledge claims, the criteria by which they are evaluated, and their relationship to the established paradigm of discovery-based research. This article argues that the recognition of these expanded forms of scholarship implies a shift toward an action research paradigm, characterized by its own epistemic norms, which stand in tension with the prevailing model of technical rationality embedded in research universities. Drawing on an analysis of MIT’s Project Athena, the discussion illustrates how such an approach operates in practice and examines the broader epistemological, institutional, and political implications of adopting this reconfigured model of scholarship.
