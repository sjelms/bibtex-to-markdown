> Smith, Adam. 1776. “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations.” 

> [!abstract]
> This section of the work examines the foundations of economic development, productivity, and the growth of national wealth. Across the first three books, the central themes are the relationship between labor, capital, and institutional structures in shaping economic outcomes. The text argues that specialization and the division of labor significantly increase productive capacity, while market systems coordinate economic activity through self-interest and exchange. Capital accumulation, investment, and the productive use of resources are presented as essential drivers of economic expansion, with money serving primarily as a mechanism that facilitates exchange rather than constituting wealth itself. The discussion also distinguishes forms of labor and capital according to their contribution to value creation. A historical analysis of economic development further argues that institutional arrangements and social structures strongly influence prosperity, showing how feudal systems constrained agricultural and economic progress, while the expansion of commerce and urban development created conditions for broader growth. Overall, the work presents a framework in which productivity, investment, market coordination, and institutional change jointly explain the development and wealth of nations.
