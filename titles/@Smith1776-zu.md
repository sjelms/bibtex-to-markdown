> [!bibliography]
> Smith, Adam. 1776. “An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations.” 

> [!abstract]
> This section examines political economy, the role of the state, and the institutional conditions necessary for economic prosperity. The text critiques prevailing economic systems, particularly mercantilism, arguing that national wealth derives from productive labour and the circulation of goods and services rather than the accumulation of precious metals. It rejects the view of trade as a zero-sum process and instead presents free exchange as mutually beneficial, increasing efficiency through specialization and the productive allocation of capital. The discussion further argues that monopolies, tariffs, and restrictive trade policies distort markets and privilege narrow interests over broader social welfare. While supporting economic freedom, the text also defines a necessary but limited role for government in maintaining national defence, administering justice, and providing public works and institutions that markets alone cannot effectively supply. It also establishes principles of fair taxation centred on proportionality, certainty, convenience, and efficiency, while warning that excessive public debt and reliance on borrowing create long-term risks for economic stability and state finances.
