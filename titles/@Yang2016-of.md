> Yang, Zizhou, Guanyu Xun. 2016. “Expansive-Restrictive Learning: A Framework for Workplace Learning Research.” [[开放教育研究]]. https://ericdata.com/tw/detail.aspx?no=258837

> [!abstract]
> This article introduces and interprets the expansive–restrictive continuum as a conceptual framework for analyzing learning processes in the workplace. Drawing primarily on the work of Alison Fuller and Lorna Unwin, the authors explain how workplace environments shape opportunities for skill development, participation, and professional growth. Rather than treating workplace learning as an incidental by-product of employment, the framework conceptualizes work itself as a structured learning environment whose characteristics can either enable or constrain learning. The paper outlines the theoretical foundations of the expansive–restrictive continuum and situates it within broader scholarship on workplace learning and vocational education. In expansive learning environments, workers are provided with diverse opportunities for participation, access to multiple sources of expertise, and the ability to move across tasks and roles. Such environments support knowledge sharing, boundary crossing, and the development of occupational competence over time. In contrast, restrictive learning environments limit participation to narrowly defined tasks, emphasize procedural compliance, and reduce opportunities for interaction and reflection, thereby constraining the development of broader professional capabilities. Building on these theoretical distinctions, the article discusses how the expansive–restrictive continuum can be used as an analytical tool for evaluating workplace learning systems, particularly in contexts such as vocational education, apprenticeship training, and organizational skill development. The authors review key characteristics that distinguish expansive and restrictive environments, including the structure of work tasks, access to mentoring or guidance, opportunities for collaboration, and the extent to which learning is integrated into everyday work practices. The paper further considers the implications of this framework for the design and improvement of vocational education and training systems. By identifying the organizational and social conditions that support expansive learning, the framework provides a basis for assessing how workplaces can better support the development of workers’ skills and occupational identities. The authors argue that the expansive–restrictive continuum offers a valuable perspective for researchers and practitioners seeking to understand the relationship between work organization, learning opportunities, and long-term workforce development. Overall, the article positions the expansive–restrictive continuum as a useful theoretical lens for examining workplace learning across different institutional and industrial contexts. It highlights the importance of workplace structure, social interaction, and participation in shaping how workers learn and develop expertise within contemporary systems of vocational training and employment.
